How to calculate lead value

Lead value is what a single lead is worth to your business, on average, before you spend a dollar acquiring it. The formula is simple:

Value per lead = Average deal value × Lead-to-customer rate

If your average deal is worth $2,500 and 8% of leads become customers, each lead is worth $2,500 × 0.08 = $200 in revenue terms. That number alone is useful, but it overstates what you can actually afford to spend — it doesn't account for the cost of delivering the product or service. For that, you need profit per lead:

Profit per lead = Value per lead × Gross margin

At a 60% gross margin, that $200 lead is worth $200 × 0.60 = $120 in gross profit. Finally, decide how much return you need on your acquisition spend — a target multiple — and divide:

Max CPL = Profit per lead ÷ Target return multiple

Targeting a 3x return means $120 ÷ 3 = $40 is the most you should pay per lead. Pay less and your margin improves further; pay more and you're eating into the profit that's supposed to fund growth, salaries, and the next campaign.

Why max CPL matters more than benchmark CPL

Our CPL calculator shows you what you're actually paying and how that compares to industry averages — useful for diagnosing a channel. But an "average" CPL for your industry tells you nothing about whether a lead is profitable for your business specifically. Two companies selling similar products can have wildly different max CPLs if their deal values, close rates, or margins differ.

Max CPL flips the question around: instead of asking "is this expensive?", it asks "can I afford this and still hit my return target?" That's the number a paid-acquisition budget should actually be built around. Anything you pay below max CPL is upside; anything above it is a channel that's quietly losing money even while "generating leads."

Worked example

Say you run a mid-market software business. Average deal value is $2,500/year, 8% of demo requests turn into customers, gross margin is 60% after hosting and support costs, and leadership wants at least a 3x return on acquisition spend before a channel is considered worth scaling.

  • Value per lead: $2,500 × 8% = $200
  • Profit per lead: $200 × 60% = $120
  • Max CPL at 3x: $120 ÷ 3 = $40

If your current CPL from paid search is $28, you're comfortably under the ceiling and can consider scaling spend. If it's $55, the channel is over budget even though it may be delivering plenty of leads — a case where volume is masking a profitability problem. Once you know your max CPL, run the actual campaign numbers through the ROI calculator to see total return across your full spend, not just per lead.

Revenue value vs. profit value

It's tempting to stop at "value per lead" (the revenue figure) and use that to set your budget. Don't — revenue isn't cash you keep. Margin matters because it strips out the cost of actually serving the customer: cost of goods, delivery, support, hosting, commissions, whatever applies to your business. A business with thin 20% margins can afford to pay far less per lead than one with 80% margins, even if their deal values and close rates are identical.

If you skip margin entirely (or leave it at 100%), this calculator simply treats value per lead and profit per lead as the same number — useful for a quick gut check, but risky for setting an actual spend ceiling. When in doubt, use a conservative margin estimate; overpaying for leads is a much easier mistake to make than underpaying.

Frequently asked questions

What is a good lead value?

There's no universal number — it depends entirely on your deal value and close rate. A $50 lead value and a $5,000 lead value can both be "good" if the resulting max CPL comfortably beats what you're actually paying. Judge lead value relative to your acquisition costs, not against other companies' numbers.

How do I find my close rate?

Pull it from your CRM: customers won ÷ leads created over the same period, ideally measured over a full sales cycle so you're not counting leads that haven't had time to convert yet. If you don't track this today, a CRM like the one recommended below is the fastest way to start — without it, every number in this calculator is a guess.

Should I use revenue or profit to set max CPL?

Profit. Revenue-based value per lead is a useful intermediate step, but setting your spend ceiling on revenue alone ignores the cost of delivering what you sold. Always apply gross margin before dividing by your target return multiple — otherwise you'll systematically overpay for leads.

Does this work for long sales cycles?

Yes, with one adjustment: use a close rate measured over the full length of your sales cycle, not a partial snapshot. If deals typically take six months to close, a close rate calculated after 60 days will understate true conversion and make your lead value — and therefore your max CPL — look artificially low. Segment by cohort (leads created in a given month) and wait for cycles to mature before trusting the rate.
