How to calculate lead generation ROI

Campaign ROI answers one question: for every dollar spent, how much profit came back? The formula is:

ROI = (Revenue × Gross margin − Spend) ÷ Spend × 100

Revenue itself builds from three inputs: leads generated, the percentage that turn into paying customers, and the average value of a closed deal. Multiply those together and you get projected revenue. That revenue then gets multiplied by gross margin — because revenue isn't profit — and spend is subtracted to find the actual dollar return before dividing by spend again to express it as a percentage.

Why gross margin belongs in the ROI formula

A lot of "ROI" calculators actually compute revenue ROI — (Revenue − Spend) ÷ Spend — and it overstates results badly. If you spend $10,000 to generate $48,000 in booked revenue, a naive revenue-ROI calculation says 380%. But if fulfilling that revenue costs 40 cents of every dollar, your real profit is $28,800, not $48,000, and true ROI drops to 188%. Still a great result — but the gap between 380% and 188% is the difference between a decision made on real numbers and one made on vanity metrics. Any campaign math that skips margin is quietly assuming your cost of delivery is zero, which it never is.

ROI vs. ROAS — they are not the same thing

ROAS (return on ad spend) is usually reported as revenue ÷ spend, with no margin applied and no distinction between a $10 impulse purchase and a $50,000 enterprise contract. It's a useful top-of-funnel efficiency signal for channels like e-commerce, where margin is fairly constant across orders. ROI, by contrast, is a profit measure — it accounts for what it actually costs you to deliver on the revenue you generated. For lead generation specifically, where "revenue" is a projection built from a close rate that hasn't fully played out yet, ROI is the more honest number to make budget decisions with. If you want the top-of-funnel efficiency view first, start with the cost per lead calculator to see what you're paying before it converts.

Worked example

Take the calculator's defaults: $10,000 spend produces 200 leads at an 8% close rate, with a $3,000 average deal and 60% gross margin. That's 16 customers, $48,000 in revenue, and $28,800 in gross profit — a $18,800 net gain on $10,000 spent, or 188% ROI. The actual cost per lead here is $50 ($10,000 ÷ 200), while the break-even CPL — the highest you could pay per lead and still hit $0 profit — works out to $144 (deal value × close rate × margin). That $94 of headroom is your margin for error if costs creep up or close rate softens.

Common mistakes that distort ROI

  • Wrong attribution window. Crediting a campaign's revenue based on a 7-day click window when your sales cycle runs 60–90 days will always undercount ROI. Match the window to how prospects actually buy.
  • Ignoring sales cost. Marketing-sourced leads still need SDRs and AEs to close them. If sales comp isn't reflected somewhere in your margin assumption, ROI is inflated.
  • Measuring too early in long cycles. Judging a campaign's ROI three weeks after launch, when deals take four months to close, tells you almost nothing — you're measuring pipeline, not results. Report leads and pipeline value early, and hold the ROI verdict until enough of the cohort has had time to close.

Once you know what a converted lead is worth to you, it's worth working that number backward — see the lead value calculator to price what you should be willing to pay per lead before you ever look at ROI.

Frequently asked questions

What is a good marketing ROI?

A common rule of thumb is that a 5:1 revenue-to-spend ratio (roughly 400% revenue ROI, before margin) is "good" and 10:1 is exceptional — but those benchmarks are frequently misapplied without margin. On a true profit basis, anything solidly above 0% is adding value; 100–300% profit ROI is a strong result for most lead gen programs once delivery costs are factored in.

What's the difference between ROI and ROAS?

ROAS is revenue divided by spend — a gross efficiency metric with no margin applied. ROI subtracts spend from margin-adjusted revenue before dividing, so it reflects actual profit. Two campaigns can have identical ROAS and very different ROI if their margins differ.

How long should I wait before measuring ROI?

At least one full average sales cycle for the cohort of leads in question, ideally longer if a meaningful share of deals close later than average. Measuring before that mostly tells you about lead volume and early pipeline, not final ROI.

Should I include salaries in the ROI calculation?

For comparing channels or campaigns against each other, keep salaries out and use direct costs plus gross margin — it keeps the comparison fair. For a company-wide view of whether marketing is paying for itself, use fully loaded costs including marketing and sales headcount.
