    Why your subject line is the highest-leverage test in email

    Of everything you test in an email campaign, the subject line moves the needle hardest per minute of effort. It's the single biggest lever on open rate, and open rate gates everything downstream — a brilliant offer inside an email nobody opens generates zero leads. Body copy, CTA placement, and send time all matter, but they only get a chance to work once the subject line has already done its job. That's why it deserves a dedicated pass before every send, not a guess typed in as an afterthought.

    What actually drives opens

    The subject line never works alone. Inbox clients show three things together: sender name, subject line, and preview text (the snippet pulled from the email's first line or a hidden preheader). A strong subject paired with a blank or redundant preview text is a wasted opportunity — the preview text is free real estate that either repeats the subject or extends it with a second reason to open. If your sender name is a generic no-reply address, no subject line trick will fully offset the trust deficit. Treat all three as one unit: recognizable sender, clear subject, complementary preview.

    How long should a subject line be?

    The 30–55 character range is the safest target. Below about 15 characters, a subject usually doesn't carry enough information to justify an open. Above roughly 70 characters, it starts getting clipped — iPhone Mail shows around 35–40 characters in the list view, the Gmail app on Android trims to about 40–50, and even generous desktop clients cut off well before 100. Anything important should sit in the first 40 characters so it survives truncation everywhere.

    Do spam filters still care about trigger words?

    Less than they used to, but not zero. In 2026, filtering is dominated by sender reputation and engagement signals — opens, replies, "delete without opening," and spam-button clicks — rather than a static keyword blocklist. A well-established sender with an engaged list can get away with words that would sink a cold domain. That said, trigger words like "guaranteed," "act now," or a string of dollar signs still correlate with lower engagement, and for senders with a thin or unproven reputation, that lower engagement is exactly the signal that erodes deliverability over time. Avoiding them costs nothing and protects the senders who need it most.

    Curiosity vs. clarity

    Media and consumer marketers lean on curiosity gaps — withholding the payoff to force a click. For B2B lead gen, clarity usually wins instead. Your recipient is scanning a work inbox, mildly skeptical, and deciding in under a second whether this is worth their attention. "How {FirstName} can cut cost per lead by 30%" outperforms a vague tease like "You won't believe this" for that audience, because it answers the only question that matters — is this relevant to my job — before the click.

    Frequently asked questions

    What is a good open rate?

    It depends heavily on list size, industry, and whether you're mailing a cold list or an engaged one. As a rough guide, 20–30% is solid for B2B lead gen sends to an opted-in list, above 35% is strong, and under 15% usually points to a list hygiene or sender reputation problem rather than a subject line problem.

    Do emojis help or hurt subject lines?

    They can help by drawing the eye in a crowded inbox, but the effect is audience-dependent and fades with overuse — the fifth email with a 🔥 in the subject this week gets ignored. For B2B lead gen, use at most one emoji, only when it adds real meaning, and always test it against a plain-text version rather than assuming it helps.

    Does personalization really work?

    Yes, but the effect size depends on what's personalized. A first-name token alone gives a modest lift and is easy for readers to tune out once every marketer does it. Personalization based on behavior or firmographic data — company name, role, a stat specific to their industry — tends to outperform a bare {FirstName} token because it signals the email was actually relevant, not just mail-merged.

    How many subject lines should I A/B test?

    Two at a time. Testing three or more splits your sample size and takes longer to reach statistical significance, which matters more than it sounds — a "winner" declared on too few sends is often just noise. Write your best two candidates, split the list evenly, and run the results through the A/B calculator before you commit to one for the full send.
