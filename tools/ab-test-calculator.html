What “statistical significance” actually means

Statistical significance answers one narrow question: if variant A and variant B actually performed identically, how likely is it that random chance alone produced a gap this big in your data? That likelihood is the p-value. A p-value of 0.03 means there's roughly a 3% chance you'd see a difference this large (or larger) purely from noise, even with no real effect. It doesn't tell you the size of the effect, whether it will hold up next month, or whether it's practically meaningful for your business — it only tells you whether you can currently rule out coincidence.

Why “B looks 10% better” after 40 conversions means nothing

Small sample sizes produce huge, meaningless swings. If variant A gets 20 conversions and variant B gets 22, B is "10% better" — but that gap is well within what random noise produces on its own. Flip a coin 20 times and you'll often get runs that look like a pattern. The fix isn't to eyeball the percentage difference; it's to run the numbers through a proper test. This calculator uses a two-proportion z-test, the standard method for comparing conversion rates between two groups, so you get an actual answer instead of a gut feeling dressed up as data.

The peeking problem

The single most common way marketers fool themselves with A/B tests is "peeking" — checking results every day and stopping the moment the test crosses your significance threshold. Every time you peek and make a stop/continue decision, you get another chance for a random fluctuation to look significant. Run enough peeks and you'll eventually see a "significant" result on a test with zero real effect a large share of the time, even though your threshold says 5%. The fix: decide your sample size and test duration in advance, then look once at the end. If you must monitor along the way, treat early "significant" readings as informational, not a stop signal.

How long should you run a test?

Two rules matter more than any calculator: run in full week increments (traffic and conversion behavior shift by day of week — a 4-day test overweights whichever days it caught), and don't call a test before you hit a reasonable minimum sample per variant, generally a few hundred conversions per variant if you can get them. If your required sample size estimate below implies months of runtime, the test as designed can't detect a difference that small in a reasonable timeframe — consider testing a bigger, bolder change instead of a minor tweak.

One-tailed vs. two-tailed

This calculator uses a two-tailed test, which checks whether B is different from A in either direction (better or worse) rather than assuming in advance that B can only win. Two-tailed is the more conservative, more defensible choice for marketing tests, since you rarely know for certain which way a change will move behavior before you run it. If you see A/B tools reporting significance you can't replicate, a one-tailed test on their end is a common culprit — it makes it easier to declare a winner.

Once you've confirmed a real winner, make sure the campaign math still works — check the new conversion rate's impact on cost per lead, and if the test was on an email send, run your subject lines through the subject line tester before your next campaign.

Frequently asked questions

What confidence level should I use?

95% is the standard default for marketing tests and what most tools use out of the box. Use 90% only for low-stakes decisions where acting on a false positive costs little. Reserve 99% for high-stakes, hard-to-reverse changes (like a full site redesign) where you want to be very sure before committing.

What if my test is "almost" significant?

A p-value of 0.06 is not "basically significant" — it's not significant. Resist the urge to round it off. Your options are to keep running the test to gather more data (this calculator estimates how much more), or to accept that the effect, if real, is too small to detect reliably with the traffic you have.

Can I test more than two variants?

Not with this calculator — it's built for a single A vs. B comparison. Testing three or more variants at once (A/B/C/D tests) requires correcting your significance threshold for the extra comparisons, or the false-positive rate creeps up with every variant you add. Run one calculator comparison per pair, or use dedicated multivariate testing software.

Does this work for email subject lines?

Yes — the same math applies to any two-option test with a binary outcome (opened vs. didn't, clicked vs. didn't). Enter your recipient counts as "visitors" and opens or clicks as "conversions." For help drafting the subject lines themselves, see the subject line tester.
